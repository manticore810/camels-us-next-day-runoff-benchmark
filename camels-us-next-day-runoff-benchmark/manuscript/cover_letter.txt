Dear Editor,

Please consider our manuscript, “Benchmarking interpretable machine-learning models for next-day runoff prediction across a 50-basin CAMELS-US subset,” for publication in the Journal of Hydrology.

This manuscript presents a chronology-respecting retrospective hindcast benchmark for one-day-ahead runoff prediction using open CAMELS-US data. Daily Daymet basin-mean forcing, USGS discharge, and static catchment attributes were assembled for a 50-basin benchmark subset spanning 1990–2014. Four models were compared under a fixed out-of-sample temporal split: persistence, Ridge regression, Random Forest, and HistGradientBoosting.

Random Forest achieved the strongest pooled test performance (NSE = 0.670, KGE = 0.756, RMSE = 2.177 mm d^-1, MAE = 0.569 mm d^-1), and basin-wise summaries reinforced the same ranking. At the same time, persistence remained highly competitive, particularly at one-day lead time, indicating that hydrologic memory remains a dominant source of short-range predictability. We believe the manuscript will be of interest to readers concerned with hydrologic forecasting, benchmark design, and reproducible machine-learning evaluation.

This revised package was strengthened in response to peer-review feedback. The manuscript now clarifies that the study is a hindcast benchmark rather than a latency-constrained operational forecast; it adds a reproducibility specification, explicit hyperparameters, stronger basin-wise statistical reporting, KGE component reporting, uncertainty summaries, a predictor glossary, and a more restrained interpretation of feature importance.

The manuscript is original, is not under consideration elsewhere, and has been approved for submission by all authors.
